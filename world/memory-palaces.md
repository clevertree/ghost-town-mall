## St. Augustine's "Mansion of the Soul"

The Christian theologian St. Augustine, familiar with the memory techniques of ancient orators, used the metaphor of the soul as a vast mansion in his *Confessions*. He described the mind as an expansive, internal space where one could find memories and, ultimately, encounter God.

## St. Teresa of Ávila's Interior Castle

A 16th-century Spanish mystic and Carmelite nun, St. Teresa wrote *The Interior Castle*, a classic work of Christian spirituality. She uses the extended metaphor of the soul as a castle with seven sets of dwelling places or mansions that a person must pass through in their spiritual journey to achieve union with God in the innermost chamber. This is a direct spiritual application of the ancient memory palace metaphor.

## Medieval Monks

Monks and priests used memory palaces to memorize extensive religious texts, including the Bible. The process of organizing and dwelling on these texts in their minds was considered a form of meditation and spiritual practice known as *Lectio Divina* (Divine Reading).

## Puritans and Preaching

Some preachers explored using the technique to memorize sermons, viewing it as a disciplined way to internalize and deliver God's word without notes, though some debated the use of the vivid imagery it required as potentially "profane."

## Giulio Camillo's "Memory Theatre"

A famous 16th-century Italian philosopher, Giulio Camillo designed a literal, physical "Theatre of Memory" that was also a complex mental system. His goal was not just to remember things, but to access "eternal knowledge" and connect the soul to celestial consciousness by using specific symbols placed in the structure.

## Thematic Relevance

The mall itself functions as a memory palace — a physical space layered with spiritual meaning, where rooms/stores become "dwelling places" on a journey inward. The ghosts inhabit a structure that mirrors these metaphors of the soul.
